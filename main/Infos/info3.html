Don’t Get Fooled Again: Outsmarting AI Lies in Philippine Politics

Ethical online behavior has never been more important, especially now that AI-generated content spreads rapidly across Philippine social media. Deepfake videos, fabricated quotes, and AI-written political posts circulate daily, often crafted to look so real that many users — particularly older generations — mistake them for legitimate information. Because these AI-made posts often appeal to emotions like fear, anger, or admiration, people may share them quickly without verifying their authenticity. This lack of digital responsibility contributes to the wide and fast spread of misinformation.

AI-driven deception has already been used in the political arena. Anonymous pages have circulated AI-generated speeches supposedly from Leni Robredo, AI-edited images portraying Ferdinand Marcos Jr. announcing false policies, or AI-written posts claiming endorsements involving figures like Sara Duterte. These politicians had no involvement in the false content, but their identities were used to manipulate public perception. Many older Filipinos, who rely heavily on Facebook and Messenger for news, were among the most easily misled. This demonstrates how vulnerable users can unintentionally harm public discourse when ethical online behavior is not practiced.

Practicing ethical online behavior means pausing, verifying, and questioning the content before sharing it — especially when it contains political statements or sensational claims. With AI tools now capable of producing highly realistic fake content, critical thinking is no longer optional; it is a civic duty. Ethical behavior online includes checking official pages, reading fact-checks, and being cautious of posts that seem too dramatic or too convenient. These steps help prevent misinformation from spreading and protect communities from manipulation.

Ultimately, digital literacy and ethical online behavior must work hand in hand. Technology will continue to advance, and AI-generated misinformation will only become more convincing. By cultivating responsible habits — verifying sources, educating older family members, and refusing to share unverified content — Filipinos can help create a more truthful and respectful online environment. Ethical online behavior is not just a personal virtue; it is a collective safeguard that protects the integrity of political discussions and strengthens the country’s democratic health.
